DIY tuckpointing can save money on small projects, but it’s not always the right choice. The key is knowing **how much of your wall is actually failing**, how high the work is, and whether your brick can tolerate DIY mistakes.

> **Scope first:** Measure your wall and estimate failing joints using our guide: [How to Measure Wall Area for Tuckpointing](/learn/how-to-measure-wall-area-for-tuckpointing). Then calculate mortar and time in the [Tuckpointing Material + Labor Calculator](/tools/tuckpointing-calculator).

## When DIY Tuckpointing Makes Sense

DIY is reasonable when:

- The wall is **one story or ground level**.
- Mortar is failing in **small sections** (roughly under 30–50 sq ft).
- Bricks are solid (not spalling or cracked).
- You can work safely without scaffolding.

In these cases, tuckpointing is mostly a careful mortar‑replacement job.

## When You Should Hire a Pro

Call a mason if any of these are true:

- **Two‑story or higher walls** (safety + staging).
- **Historic or soft brick** that can chip easily.
- Bricks are failing (spalling, bulging, loose bricks).
- You see structural movement or stair‑step cracking.
- More than ~50% of joints are failing.

If bricks are failing, read: [Tuckpointing vs Brick Replacement](/learn/tuckpointing-vs-brick-replacement).

## What DIYers Underestimate

### Time

Most first‑time DIYers underestimate how long mortar removal takes. A useful planning number is **8–12 sq ft per hour** for beginners.

Use the [Tuckpointing Calculator](/tools/tuckpointing-calculator) to see a realistic labor range for your wall.

### Materials

Mortar bags are cheap — but mixing the right type matters. Type N is common for residential brick; Type S is stronger but may be too hard for older brick.

### Weather

Avoid:

- Freezing temps (mortar won’t cure)
- Direct hot sun (mortar dries too fast)
- Wet walls (bad bond)

Spring and fall are usually ideal.

## A Simple DIY Plan

1. Remove failing mortar to ~3/4" depth.
2. Brush joints clean.
3. Lightly mist the wall.
4. Pack fresh mortar firmly.
5. Tool joints to match existing profile.
6. Keep mortar damp for 2–3 days to cure.
